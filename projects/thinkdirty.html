Think Dirty App

Think Dirty helps users assess the safety of personal care and household products by rating their ingredients from 1 to 10. It empowers users to become conscious-consumers and educates them to make safer and “cleaner” shopping decisions.

Think Dirty's current website (2025)

Objective

As someone who genuinely wanted to use the Think Dirty app, I was very disappointed. Browsing and searching for products was frustrating; Almost every page overwhelmed me with information; And it didn’t match the company website’s quirky aesthetic. Think Dirty’s poor design makes not only the app, but the entire brand look unprofessional and untrustworthy.

So, I took it upon myself to redesign their app, starting with their form for submitting products. This form allows users to send information about a product to the company so it can be rated and added to their database.

Issues of Think Dirty's From Design
  1. Colours do not match company's branding
  2. Inconsistent labelling of input fields
  3. Users must scroll to top to submit
  4. Too many category options to choose from
  5. Completing the form takes too long
  6. Forces users to complete unnecessary tasks
Design Iterations

I designed multiple iterations of the form, testing each one against the original design. Consistently, the majority of participants preferred my iterations over the original in terms of speed, understandability, and overall design. However, a common critique I kept receiving was that my design didn’t look as good as the original. Its usability was better, but it didn’t look as “sleek,” “modern” or “trustworthy.” For one iteration, a participant said it looked “really amateur.” While remaining faithful to the brand’s aesthetic, I experimented with the UI until I no longer got those comments.

Wizard or Scroll?

In my research, I found some participants felt dividing the form in three pages was less overwhelming and easier to understand, but others preferred the one page of the original design because it gave them more control and a better idea of how long it would take to complete. Some also felt it was quicker, even though mine had fewer clicks and required less information. I suspected seeing one page took less effort and cognitive load than having to process multiple.

Final Design and Outcomes

According to my final usability test, my suspicions were correct. I put my design on one page and tested it against the original once more. Compared to previous tests, even more participants preferred my design over the original. Because of these results and because Think Dirty users would be completing this form in store, they may not always have access to a good Wi-Fi connection., I turned my three-page form into one.

  • 100% of participants said my design was easier to understand.
  • 91% preferred my design overall.
  • 81% said my design was faster.
